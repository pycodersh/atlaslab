## Your Brain Is Designed to Forget

Forgetting isn't a flaw. It's a feature.

Your brain receives an overwhelming amount of information every day. To function, it has to decide what to keep and what to discard. The default answer is: discard everything that hasn't proven itself useful.

This is why you can study an English pattern on Monday and have no memory of it by Friday. Your brain didn't fail you — it did exactly what it was built to do.

The question is: how do you convince your brain that a pattern is worth keeping?

---

## The Forgetting Curve

In the 1880s, German psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus discovered something that every language learner experiences but few understand.

He found that memory decays in a predictable curve. After learning something new:

- After **20 minutes**: you've forgotten 40% of it
- After **1 hour**: you've forgotten 56%
- After **1 day**: you've forgotten 67%
- After **1 week**: you've forgotten 75%
- After **1 month**: you've forgotten 79%

Without reinforcement, nearly everything disappears.

But Ebbinghaus discovered something else — something that changes everything.

---

## The Spacing Effect

Each time you successfully recall a piece of information *just before* you would have forgotten it, two things happen:

1. The memory is **reconstructed** — not just refreshed, but rebuilt stronger
2. The **forgetting curve resets** — but flatter this time, meaning you'll retain it longer before the next review

Do this enough times, and what was once a fragile new memory becomes something you simply *know* — the way you know your own name or how to ride a bike.

This is the **spacing effect**, and it's one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology.

---

## Why Most Study Methods Fight the Brain

Cramming, vocabulary lists, and grammar drills all have one thing in common: they work against the spacing effect.

When you study 50 words in one sitting, your brain sees no reason to consolidate them into long-term memory. You haven't proven they're worth keeping — you've just thrown them at it all at once.

The result: a burst of short-term recognition that evaporates within days.

**Passive review has the same problem.** Re-reading a pattern or hearing it in a song feels productive, but recognition is not the same as recall. You can recognize something without being able to produce it.

What actually works is **active recall at spaced intervals** — being forced to retrieve the pattern from memory right before it would have faded.
