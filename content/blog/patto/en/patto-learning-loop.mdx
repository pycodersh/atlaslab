## Why Most English Apps Don't Work

You've tried the apps. You've done the streaks. You've collected points.

But six months later, you still hesitate when you need to actually speak.

The problem isn't motivation. The problem is the method. Most apps treat English like a collection of facts to memorize — vocabulary words, grammar rules, isolated phrases.

That's not how language works in your brain.

---

## How Patto Is Different: The 5-Pattern Story

Every story in Patto is built around **5 core English patterns** — not random vocabulary, not grammar drills, but the exact chunks of language that native speakers use automatically.

Here's what the loop looks like:

### Step 1: Read the Story
You read a short, engaging story written entirely in natural English. The 5 patterns appear inside the story the way they'd appear in real life — with context, emotion, and meaning attached.

Your brain doesn't just see the pattern. It *experiences* it.

### Step 2: Hide & Recall
After reading, Patto covers each pattern and asks you to produce it from memory.

This is the most important step. The act of *retrieving* information — not just recognizing it — is what moves language from passive knowledge to active ability.

### Step 3: Repeat Across Stories
The same pattern doesn't just appear once. It comes back in different stories, different contexts, different emotional situations.

By the time you've finished a set of stories, each pattern has appeared enough times that it starts to feel familiar — not memorized, but *known*.

### Step 4: Spaced Review
Patto's SRS engine tracks when each pattern is about to fade from memory and surfaces it right before that happens.

This means you're never reviewing things you already know cold — only the patterns that need one more encounter to lock in permanently.

### Step 5: Write & Get Feedback
Once you've learned a set of patterns, Patto asks you to use them in your own writing. You submit a short essay or response, and AI reviews it — not just for errors, but for how naturally you're using the patterns in your own voice.

---

## Why 5 Patterns Per Story?

Five is deliberate.

Too few and there's not enough new input per session. Too many and the cognitive load prevents deep processing — you end up skimming instead of absorbing.

Five patterns per story is the sweet spot: enough to make real progress, few enough that each one gets the attention it needs to stick.

After 100 stories, you'll have encountered **500 patterns** — the core set that covers the vast majority of natural, fluent English conversation.

---

## The Compounding Effect

Here's what most learners miss: language learning isn't linear. It compounds.

Each pattern you internalize makes the next one easier to absorb. The context from one story bleeds into the next. Your brain starts building a mental model of how English actually works — not as a set of rules, but as a living system.

That's the Patto learning loop. And it works because it's designed around how your brain actually learns — not how textbooks assume it does.
